The Data Commons Project

2009

Summary

During 2010, the Data Commons Project under the auspices of the Cooperative Development Institute was supported by a grant of $3500 from Equal Exchange, and donations from the public.

The goal of the Data Commons Project is to develop and maintain an accurate, comprehensive, public database of cooperative economic initiatives. We believe this could help achieve the vision of Equal Exchange's worker-owners of “A vibrant mutually cooperative community of two million committed participants trading fairly one billion dollars a year in a way that transforms the world.”

During 2010, DCP developers did the following:

  • Released all DCP software public and under an OSI-approved license.
  • Collaborated in a code sprint to improve an online directory.
  • Investigated data licensing and privacy issues.
  • Investigated database issues.
  • Contributed some fixes for software used in the course of their work back to their creators: specifically Gnumeric and Sqlite.
    • .

Our policy was for all development to be done by committed members of the DCP, without outsourcing. Developers were free to invoice at a fixed rate, and were encouraged to exercise their discretion about whether they needed to invoice in order to justify getting DCP work onto their calendar.

Free and open software

For the DCP to succeed, we will need a lot of partners to trust us and collaborate with us. As one step towards earning that trust, we have decided to make our software and our software development process public.

On May 4, 2009, DCP software development was migrated to a public site:

http://launchpad.net/datacommons

This site was key to the operation of a code sprint in October 2009. All DCP code is released under an OSI-approved license, making it free and open source.

Online directory

The DCP has for several years maintained a public index of cooperative-spirited organizations at find.coop. A partial rewrite of this site was developed at a code sprint held in Brattleboro in March 2007, but was never completed. During 2009 the “source code” for this site was publically released on launchpad, a well-respected site for software collaboration. This code was further improved during a code sprint in October 2009. A live test site (with minimal data) can be seen at:

http://proto.find.coop

The new code is much sounder and easier to maintain. It also offers some new features:

Neighborhood mapping

This was a popular feature on find.coop: the ability to visualize cooperative organizations that neighbor each other. This was ported to proto.find.coop and improved to be more maintainable and scalable.

Cooperative-minded mapping

find.coop currently shows maps of organization locations using the Google Maps viewer and data. The Google Maps service is of excellent quality, but we realized that the proprietary nature of the viewer and data was distasteful to some of the community we wished to serve.

Therefore for proto.find.coop, we have instead used free and open alternatives to the Google Maps viewer and data -- "openlayers" and "openstreetmap" respectively. We make it easy for users to switch to Google Maps if that is their preference, but we don't make that choice for them.

Machine tagging

An experimental branch has implemented custom fields for organizations in a systematic way using a method called "machine tags" or "triple tag". This is intended to allow fields defined by different authorities that might not quite coincide to coexist in the directory (although a long term goal of the project would be reconciliation). There is no consensus among DCP members about whether this approach is the right one is not, and so it is maintained separately.

Bulk download

In proto.find.coop, it is much easier to download the results of a search as a spreadsheet. Both search results and individual listings are available for download in structured formats (currently CSV and XML).

Data licensing and privacy

Just like the DCP software is free and open, we would like to make databases of cooperative initiatives free and open. As for software, it is possible to make creative use of existing laws to preserve the freedom of data.

The first ever open data license was released in 2008 by a project run by the Open Knowledge Foundation (called, coincidentally, the "Open Data Commons"). During 2009, they released an Open Database License that is close to what the DCP needs:

http://www.opendatacommons.org/licenses/odbl/summary/

Further drafting efforts are in the works. Given the limiting funding of the DCP, we are happy to see a complementary project dealing with the legal issues involved in licensing data correctly.

Privacy is another matter. We are learning that many groups are initially enthusiastic about making their data open, but then balk as the implications sink in. One common problem is that for any data related to people, organizations quite rightly want to protect their members from having their data used in ways they haven't expressly allowed. Postfix experiment.

Contributed fixes

Gnumeric

Gnumeric is a free and open spreadsheet program that is an alternative to Excel. In the course of evaluating Gnumeric for its utility in importing and exporting user data in bulk from spreadsheets, a DCP developer improved the codebase. The improvement was submitted “upstream” to the Gnumeric team on May 6, 2009, and was accepted in modified form.

https://bugzilla.gnome.org/show_bug.cgi?id=581616
http://projects.gnome.org/gnumeric/announcements/1.9/gnumeric-1.9.8.shtml

Gnumeric is licensed under the GNU General Public License (GPLv2), one of the first licenses to use copyright law creatively in order to preserve the freedom of users rather than to limit them (an approach now called "copyleft").

Sqlite

Sqlite is a public domain database that is probably the most widely deployed SQL database engine in the world (it is embedded in firefox, skype, and many cellphones, for example). In the course of evaluating Sqlite for its utility in importing and exporting user data in bulk from databases, a DCP developer fixed a flaw in round-tripping strings containing numbers with leading zeros (for example, certain zip codes). The improvement was submitted “upstream” to the Sqlite team on June 11, 2009, and was accepted in modified form.

http://www.sqlite.org/cvstrac/tktview?tn=3906
http://www.sqlite.org/src/info/7b61b6c6ce

The Sqlite codebase has been dedicated to the public domain by all its contributors, and so can be used without a license. Rather subversively, in the place where source code would usually contain licensing information, the Sqlite authors instead have the following blessing: 

    May you do good and not evil
    May you find forgiveness for yourself and forgive others
    May you share freely, never taking more than you give.

Advocacy

The DCP acted as a voice for the free and open source approach, convincing at least one peer project to switch from mandating a closed technologyto requiring an open one.
